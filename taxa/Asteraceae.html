Asteraceae (family)

Asteraceae (alternatively called Compositae), the sunflower, daisy, and thistle family, is quite possibly the most widespread and highly speciated families of flowering plants, composed of at least 23,600 species [4] across every continent except Antartica. Perhaps the most noticible distinguishing feature of this plant family is its specialized and complex floral structure. The inflorescence consists of one or more flat to conical flower heads called capitula, made up of as many as a few hundred tiny florets.

Members of the Asteraceae typically produce taproots, but occasionally have fibrous root systems [5]

Key Morphological Features

To account for the complexity and extent of variation within this family, I'll divide this section into typical morphologies of the psudanthium (false flower) and those of the individual floret.

Caliptula in the Aster family typically match one of five general designs [4]:

  1. discoid: only disk flowers; all bisexual.
  2. disciform: only disk flowers; a mixture of pistillate and sterile with bisexual and staminate, in the same or different heads.
  3. radiate: central (bisexual or male) disk flowers and peripheral (female or sterile) ray flowers.
  4. ligulate, with all ray flowers (typically with 5-toothed corolla apices).
  5. bilabiate, with all bilabiate flowers.

Features of the psudantheum:

  • Compound flower head composed of one to hundreds of individual florets [3]
  • Commonly possesses specialized ray florets with fused, ligulate petals along outer edge of capitulum. Often sterile.
  • Commonly possesses disk florets in the interior section of the capitulum.
  • Capitulum is typically subtended by one to many rows of false sepals (involucral bracts).

Features of the individual floret: [4]

  • The flowers are epigynous (female << male), bisexual or unisexual.
  • Corolla is sympetalous (united petals), with 5 (rarely 4) lobes.
  • In some subclasses, lobes are reduced to 3 teeth in ray florets.
  • Calyx often modified into a pappus of hairs or bristles.
  • Stamens are 5, whorled, with anthers fused into a tube through which the style grows.
  • Distinctive bilobed style.
  • Fruit is an achene.
